Overview

Used Car Dealer Sim puts you in the owner's chair at an independent used car store. Every unit on the ground is your money: what you paid at the block, what the shop charged to make it front-line ready, and what it costs to hold while it sits. Revenue comes from two places — the spread between acquisition cost and selling price, and the F&I office, where financed deals generate product sales and lender reserve.

The simulation is built around the numbers the trade actually runs on: MMR as the wholesale anchor, condition announcements at auction, days-in-stock pressure on retail pricing, credit-tiered lending with realistic rates and terms, and per-copy gross tracked front and back.

Systems

Auction
A daily run list with live bidding against a competitor. Each unit carries a condition light and a reconditioning estimate. Green announcements are clean; yellow and red conceal risk — the shop bill can come in well over the estimate.
Book values
Every vehicle displays MMR, trade-in, and retail. The margin is made at acquisition: the spread between your buy figure and retail, less reconditioning and holding cost, is the deal.
Retail desk
Set asking prices against retail value. Pricing discipline drives traffic; units over 30 days in stock are flagged as aged and pull fewer customers. Offers can be accepted, countered, or declined — counters carry walk risk.
Trade-ins
Roughly a third of customers bring a trade with a requested ACV. Accept the number and the unit is yours — including whatever reconditioning it actually needs.
F&I office
About 60% of customers finance. Deals clear the lender first, then reach the menu: service contract, GAP, tire & wheel, and appearance protection, each presented once. Payment, APR, term, and down payment are computed from the customer's credit profile and update live as products are added.
Credit line
Working capital at 12% APR, billed daily against the outstanding balance. The limit scales with lot capacity, from $100,000 at 10 spots to $1,000,000 at 100.

Operator's manual

Buy under MMR or don't buy

The competitor at the auction bids to a maximum derived from MMR. If you find yourself paying over the number, you are inheriting someone else's problem plus a buy fee. Walk. There is another run list tomorrow, and the discipline to pass is worth more than any single unit.

Condition lights price the risk, not the car

A red-light unit at the right money outperforms a green-light unit at the wrong money. The estimate on yellow and red announcements can run 50% over once the shop tears in, so bid as if it will. Green lights cost more at the block precisely because they carry no surprise.

Turn beats gross

Every unit accrues holding cost daily, and the credit line bills interest every morning whether you sell or not. A $1,800 front in 12 days beats a $2,600 front in 45. When a unit crosses 30 days in stock, reprice it — aged inventory pulls less traffic at any number, and the loss compounds.

The back end carries the store

Front-end margin on a competitively priced unit is thin. The store's real profitability lives in the F&I office: reserve on financed paper and product penetration. Service contracts convert better on high-mileage units; GAP converts best on subprime paper, which also carries the largest reserve. A subprime customer with a full menu regularly out-grosses a prime cash buyer paying closer to ask.

Manage the line like inventory

The credit line is leverage, and leverage cuts both ways. Drawn capital sitting idle is pure interest expense; drawn capital in fast-turning inventory is how the store scales. Repay when cash is heavy, draw when the run list is strong, and never let the balance ride against a lot full of aged units.
