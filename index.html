Overview

You own an independent used car store. Every unit on the lot is your capital: the purchase price at auction, the reconditioning bill, and the daily cost of holding it until it sells.

Profit comes from two places: the spread between what you paid and what you sell for, and the F&I office, where financed deals generate rate participation and product sales. The simulation uses real industry numbers — MMR at the auction, condition announcements, days-in-stock pricing pressure, credit-tiered lending with accurate payment math, and gross tracked front and back per unit.

Game systems
Auction
A daily run list with live bidding against a competitor. Condition lights price the risk: green is clean, yellow and red conceal shop bills that can run well over the announcement.
Book values
Every vehicle shows MMR, trade-in, and retail. The margin is made at acquisition — the spread between your buy figure and retail, less reconditioning and holding cost, is the deal.
Negotiation
Work the counter and the trade allowance on sliders while a live sentiment gauge shows how the customer is taking it. Push too hard and they walk.
F&I office
Roughly 60% of customers finance. Deals clear the lender, contract two points over the buy rate, and hit the menu: configurable service contracts, GAP, tire & wheel, and appearance protection, with payments computed from the customer's actual credit profile.
Credit line
Working capital at 12% APR billed daily against the balance. The limit scales with lot capacity, from $100,000 at 10 spots to $1,000,000 at 100.
Staffing
After the first expansion, hire a Used Car Buyer at $1,000 per day to work the sale for you — buys under MMR, avoids red lights, caps at three units a day.
Strategy

Buying at auction

The competing bidder's maximum is based on MMR. Paying over MMR means overpaying plus a buy fee, so pass when the bidding runs past the number — a new run list comes every day.

Condition lights

Green lights are clean. Yellow and red carry reconditioning estimates that can come in up to 50% over once the shop opens the car up, so bid assuming the worst case. A red-light car bought cheap enough is still a good buy; a green-light car bought too high is not.

Inventory turn

Every unit costs money daily to hold, and the credit line bills interest every morning. Selling for $1,800 front in 12 days beats $2,600 front in 45. Reprice anything past 30 days in stock — aged units pull fewer customers at any price.

Negotiation

Watch the sentiment gauge while setting your counter and trade allowance. A receptive customer will accept a meaningful counter; an irritated one walks over small money. Cutting the trade allowance damages sentiment faster than raising the price does.

F&I

Front margin on a competitively priced unit is thin, so most profit comes from the F&I office: participation on financed deals and product sales. Service contracts sell better on high-mileage cars, GAP sells best to subprime customers, and a financed subprime deal with products regularly out-grosses a cash deal at a higher price.

Glossary
MMR
Manheim Market Report — the wholesale benchmark. What a car is worth at auction in its current condition.
ACV
Actual Cash Value — what a trade-in is really worth to the dealer, as opposed to the allowance shown to the customer.
Front-end gross
Profit on the vehicle itself: selling price minus total cost (purchase, fees, reconditioning).
Back-end gross
Profit from the F&I office: rate participation plus margin on protection products.
PVR
Per Vehicle Retail — average total gross (front plus back) per unit sold. The core measure of how much each deal is worth.
Buy rate
The interest rate the lender approves the loan at. The dealer contracts the customer at a marked-up rate — here, two points over.
Participation
The dealer's share of the extra finance charge created by the rate markup. Paid by the lender as backend profit on financed deals.
VSC
Vehicle Service Contract — an extended coverage product sold in F&I, priced by coverage level, term, and the vehicle's age and mileage.
GAP
Coverage that pays the difference between a loan balance and an insurance payout if the car is totaled. Only relevant on financed deals.
Reconditioning
The shop work that makes an acquired unit ready to retail. "Recon" is a direct cost against the deal.
Run list
The lineup of vehicles crossing the auction block on a given day.
Days in stock
How long a unit has sat on the lot. Aged inventory (30+ days) draws fewer customers and bleeds holding cost.
